Description
It can be uncomfortable to pass by a street where you remember your enemy often spends time at. You feel like they could be right behind you at any moment, piercing you with their eyes or even worse, trying to talk to you.

Colorstory

Process

I didn't know I was going to have such a lecturer when I signed up for the Internet of Things class at Hongik University in Seoul, South Korea. David Hall had a very compelling argument to make about practical design. Is it really possible to find out exactly what the world needs by analysing every little bit and writing many post it notes? A controversial question, indeed. He told us about how Samsung worked towards creating a smart fridge. All the right steps; a detailed analysis of the target group, various prototypes and test, they spend a hefty portion of their money on this thing that had everything screaming for it's success. Everyone wanted it, no doubt. It was released and it tanked. Because truly, while everyone wanted it, nobody needed it. But how were you supposed to know? All sticky notes said yes. With this story, David concluded that there is no point trying to figure out what the world needs and analysing target groups. What is it that we truly want? He wanted us to go down to the nitty gritty of our minds, talk about our desires and worries and just go do something that is centered around something that we feel, because that is the only thing we can be certain of.

So I went for it. And one feeling that truly defines a good part of my whole being is how much I am scared to meet someone I know by chance when I'm out and about or even worse, someone I dislike or have a quarrel with. I always wished there to be a way to detect them, since I have pretty bad tunnel vision. Combining the feeling of a piercing stare and the practicality of a internet powered device I thought you could use the location data that everyone almost voluntarily spews out into the world (for example from Snapchat) to warn yourself when someone is about to approach you.

Little spikes are arranged in a grid that you attach to your back. Using yourself as the center of the coordination system, it can track someone's position on a two dimensional plane so you can go and turn around before they can even detect you. Unfortunately the class with David ended early and we weren't able to make a proper prototype.

Pure red, black and white were chosen as the thematic color scheme. Red to represent the aggression but also fear, the irritation that an unwelcome encounter brings. It's literally seeing red, like an alert that goes off in situations of danger. Black and White because there are only two options: engage or run away. There is no comfortable middle ground, either you face your enemy or you don't.

Conclusion

This concept aligns well with my asocial theory. The semester prior, I had developed a concept of an uninviting pavillion that would keep visitors away for a park in Mannheim so I could finally be at peace.

Especially in South Korea, a lot of the lifestyle seemed to predict what I felt was coming: a desire or even need to be asocial. It's nothing new that the newer generation spends less time physically with their friends, that they are more and more incapable of holding a face to face conversation. At the same time the number of bachelors as well as people living on their own have been increasing rapidly. In East Asia, it is not uncommon to see single seat tables in restaurants or streamers that do Mukbang, just eating on stream to make other people feel less lonely. And as I told more Koreans about my concept, it was met with cheers; they would love if that was something they could use. Social awkwardness is becoming more normal than ever. People are scared to commit to relationships and would probably be satisfied with sex robots. So I think I might be onto something.
