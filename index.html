Thoughts

Micro essays

I like to dwell on some questions. Feel free to send me answers or more questions.

Is advertisement over?

In the wake of adblock and generations that stop watching TV or listening to the radio altogether, ads seem to struggle more than ever to reach their goals. Even though I am in the same industry, I find myself tuning out ads more often than looking at them with interest. People my age usually consume tailored content but at the same time don't like being spied on to receive tailored ads. We prefer genuinity, which seems to be inherently contrary to the very nature of ads. At most, they don't create new customers but only reaffirm their previous customers' buying decision. At the same, time ads are the basis of all free content online. There have been changes in how companies reach their target group nowadays. They switch to influencer-based ads and implement a "personality" by uploading politically charged ads. But I don't know if that is enough. The concept of advertisment has always left a bit of a bitter aftertaste. Of course there were many ads that were genuinely enjoyable and even shaped our culture. I feel like traditional advertisement might have met its end. But then again I don't really know the numbers. If it was that ineffective, surely companies would stop pouring millions and millions into them.

Should a designer know everything?

A designer connects the user to the service or product they aim to use in a way that is accessible to them. For example, many companies use software to organise their finances or flow of products. A designer makes the software usable for them. In order to make an interface that is both usable and understandable, they need to gain the necessary knowledge. In less obvious examples, the target group might be locally confined, so the designer needs to become acquainted with the local culture (ie. designing an App for China versus Brazil.) But where is the line? Surely a designer cannot be expected to be an expert in all fields possible. Yet it is recommended to stay on top of so many topics, such as current politics, global finances, cultures from varying countries, software, languages, sciences, sociology, etc, etc, etc. Is that too much asked or just what's necessary?

What exciting things can you make with mobile first?

It's nothing new that more and more sites are viewed primarily on mobile devices. Hence it is important to make the mobile design a priority. But I found myself to feel limited when I actually design the mobile site first. It's like it quickly becomes boring, trying to make a site that works with our clumsy fingertips. What is it about this approach that still feels lacking?

Why is the value of designers so unclear to so many people?

Many designers probably have heard at least a couple hundred times that people worry for their future financial security if they become designers. It seems as though it's a secret kept from the general public that design affects almost everything and that the bigger companies become, the more they pay for good design. You will be hard pressed to ask global players like Nike, Apple or Mercedes to sell their logo. I think a big part of the misconception is that many designers themselves don't seem to know their value and how difficult their skills can be to some people. Designing can be fun, like drawing or making music. But you need abilities like effective visual communication, empathy and knowing how to use computer programs. And those aren't as easy to come by as it seems sometimes.
