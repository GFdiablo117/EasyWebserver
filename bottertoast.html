Description
Liveperson is a multinational service that provides an asynchronous messaging solution between customers and brands, using bots for chat and other solutions. They came to the faculty of software engineering to find a solution to make their services more accessible to clients who don't have as much programming experience. Combined with the skills of the design students, four teams of ten students competed to create the best solution. Our team was called Gryffindor, and that is a little spoiler for how the competition ended...

Process

We started off getting to know one of another. It was the first time for most of us to work with or even meet a student from another department, which already posed a challenge. We had to figure out how to make use of our differences and how to communicate with each other. Personally, it was important for me to attend as many meetings as possible, especially since the focus was on the software side of things. Even though I didn't fully understand what they were doing, I would still make sure to ask what they are up to and become a member they can trust.

We designers sat together to figure out a userflow: How would someone without a programming background create a bot? It took us some time to figure out what exactly a user would need to see in order to create an effective chatbot without prior knowledge. The functions were mapped out by Liveperson: create an interface where you can create a bot that can greet, answer frequently asked questions and redirect to a human service agent if the chatter wants something else. The bots must have the ability to be turned on and off, fed content and features, and be trained. We compared other bot services that offer almost identical services and learned from them, as well as figured out what can be improved upon.

Since the deal was to make it easy and not intimidating, I suggested the name Bottertoast as brand name. It references the tradition of naming software after food and also adds a little silliness that I feel like we can all use in professional life every now and then. And though it was a bold idea, it was memorable and straight forward.

Some pages were easy to make; an overview of the bots as well as a form-based set up page, were straight forward tasks. But the actual configuration of the bot was both challenging and interesting. We thought if you were making a chat, why not display it as a chat? A more powerful chat interface that allows you to create parallel responses to different requests.

From the chat concept, we moved on to implement the chatbubble theme in other areas. They became buttons and the shape of our form fields. We settled for both a bright theme as well as a dark one that is friendlier to the eye. A buttery yellow was an easy choice for obvious reasons, and a complementing dark blue balances and adds a professional feeling.

We also wanted the bots to seem less technical and more like partners. So we changed wording into "born" instead of "created" and "asleep" instead of "inactive."

Conclusion

This was my first multidisciplinary class where I could prove my skills that I have learned. I was stoked, ready to meet new people and see their perspectives. Usually team work can be quite daunting, resulting in the all too familiar "one person does everything, the rest watches" scheme of academic life. But working with the software engineers was like getting thrown into a detailed and fast paced rythm with presentations every week and trying to juggle communication between design and software engineering students as well as lecturers, professors and the client. It was a challenge, but I had the pleasure to work with a very nice team of students.

This is where I learned an often overlooked personal skill you should have when you work in a team: be nice. It sounds trivial, almost unimportant, but productiveness increases so much when you don't dread coming to the meetings. It does not mean you have to be best friends with everyone; it's okay to keep a professional distance. Howevver, being able not to take things personally, giving your team members the benefit of the doubt, and letting them explain themselves when things don't go perfectly is important. Transparent communication and understanding the hurdles that come in working with someone from a different field are necessary. I quickly realised that one of the most valued but overlooked skills is the ability of a designer to clearly express an idea visually. What you usually would need hours of discussion for can be very quickly explained in simple drawings.

Overall, this group project helped me both recognise my own unique skills as a designer as well as see what programmers do and how they think. I enjoyed this experience greatly. Oh, and we won the competition!
